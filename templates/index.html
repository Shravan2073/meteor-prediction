Project Overview

Our project simulates a solar system with planets orbiting a central star, all while meteors travel through space. The simulation tracks meteor patterns and uses statistical methods to predict future meteor activity.

This has practical applications in real-world asteroid detection and impact prediction systems used by space agencies to monitor potentially hazardous objects.

  • Bayesian inference for updating probability distributions

Theoretical Background

Markov Chains

Markov chains are mathematical systems that experience transitions from one state to another according to certain probabilistic rules. They are memoryless, meaning the next state depends only on the current state.

In our simulation, we model meteor activity as a Markov process with three states:

  • Low Activity: Few meteors entering the system
  • Medium Activity: Moderate meteor frequency
  • High Activity: Intense meteor shower

The transition matrix defines the probability of moving between states:

P = [
[0.6, 0.3, 0.1],
[0.2, 0.5, 0.3],
[0.1, 0.3, 0.6]
]

Where P[i][j] represents the probability of transitioning from state i to state j.

Bayesian Inference

Bayesian inference is a statistical method that updates the probability of a hypothesis as more evidence becomes available. It's based on Bayes' theorem:

P(A|B) = (P(B|A) × P(A)) / P(B)

In our context:

  • P(A|B) is the posterior probability - our updated belief about meteor rates after observing data
  • P(B|A) is the likelihood - probability of observing our data given a meteor rate
  • P(A) is the prior probability - our initial belief about meteor rates
  • P(B) is the evidence - normalizing constant

We use a Gamma distribution as our prior, with parameters α and β:

P(λ) = (β^α / Γ(α)) × λ^(α-1) × e^(-βλ)

As we observe meteor counts, we update our distribution to form a posterior that better reflects the true meteor arrival rate.

Integration in Our Simulation

In our solar system simulation:

  1. Markov Chain: Controls the underlying meteor activity level, which changes over time according to the transition probabilities.
  2. Celestial Physics: Meteors are affected by gravity from the central star, creating realistic trajectories.
  3. Region of Interest: We monitor a specific region of space for meteor activity to focus our analysis.
  4. Bayesian Updating: As meteors are observed, we update our posterior distribution to refine our model of meteor arrival rates.

This combined approach allows us to:

  • Model complex, time-varying meteor activity
  • Account for uncertainty in our estimates
  • Adapt our predictions as new data becomes available
  • Visualize both the physical system and statistical analysis in real-time

Mathematical Details

Markov Chain Implementation

The simulation updates the meteor activity state every 100 frames using the transition matrix:

current_state = np.random.choice([0, 1, 2], p=transition_matrix[current_state])

Each state corresponds to a different meteor generation rate:

meteor_rate = {"low": 1, "medium": 2, "high": 3}

This creates a time-varying pattern of meteor activity that mimics natural fluctuations observed in real meteor showers.

Bayesian Update Process

Our implementation uses a Gamma-Poisson conjugate model:

  1. Prior: We start with a Gamma(α, β) prior on the meteor arrival rate λ.
  2. Likelihood: The number of observed meteors follows a Poisson distribution with parameter λ.
  3. Posterior: After observing n meteors with a sum of k, our posterior is Gamma(α + k, β + n).
alpha_post = alpha_prior + sum(observed_meteors)
beta_post = beta_prior + len(observed_meteors)

As more data is collected, our posterior distribution narrows, providing a more precise estimate of the true meteor arrival rate.

Physics Model

The motion of celestial bodies and meteors is governed by simplified gravitational physics:

distance = sqrt(dx² + dy²) + 0.1 # Small offset to prevent division by zero
gravity_force = 100 / distance² # Inverse square law

# Update meteor position due to gravity
x += gravity_force * dx / distance * mass
y += speed + gravity_force * dy / distance * mass

Planets follow circular orbits, with position calculated as:

angle += speed
x = center_x + orbit_radius * cos(angle)
y = center_y + orbit_radius * sin(angle)

Moons orbit their planets in a similar fashion, but with the planet's position as the center of orbit.
